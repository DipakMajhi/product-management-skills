# UX Critique Framework

As a PM, your role in design critique is not to redesign the product - it is to evaluate whether the design solves the user's problem effectively and identify specific friction points that will affect adoption and retention.

## Jakob Nielsen's 10 Usability Heuristics (Simplified for PMs)

Apply this skill to: $ARGUMENTS


1. **Visibility of system status**: Does the user always know what is happening? (Loading states, progress indicators, confirmation messages)
2. **Match with real world**: Does the product use language and concepts the user already knows?
3. **User control and freedom**: Can users undo mistakes? Exit unwanted states easily?
4. **Consistency**: Do similar things look and behave consistently? Does the product follow platform conventions?
5. **Error prevention**: Does the design prevent mistakes before they happen?
6. **Recognition over recall**: Does the product show options rather than requiring users to remember them?
7. **Flexibility**: Can expert users use shortcuts while the interface stays simple for beginners?
8. **Aesthetic minimalism**: Does every element earn its place? Is there visual noise that distracts?
9. **Error recovery**: When errors occur, are messages clear and helpful?
10. **Help and documentation**: Is help available when needed and easy to find?

## PM-Specific Design Evaluation Criteria

Beyond heuristics, evaluate:
- **Time to first value**: How quickly does a new user experience the product's core value?
- **Activation path clarity**: Is the most important action the most obvious one?
- **Retention hooks**: Does the design create habits or reasons to return?
- **Conversion friction**: Where does the design create unnecessary barriers to upgrade or purchase?
- **Mobile experience**: Is the mobile version a first-class experience or a shrunk desktop?

## Structured Critique Format

When evaluating a design or product:
1. State what works (do not only criticize)
2. Identify specific issues by heuristic or criterion
3. For each issue: severity (Critical/Major/Minor), frequency (always/often/occasionally), and impact (blocks task completion / creates confusion / minor annoyance)
4. Suggest a direction for improvement (not a specific design solution - that is the designer's job)
