# Product Vision Crafter

A product vision is a vivid, emotionally resonant description of the future state you are working to create. It is not a mission statement, a feature list, or a business objective. It answers: "What world are we building, and for whom?"

Apply this skill to: $ARGUMENTS

## What Makes a Strong Product Vision

### The 7-Point Vision Quality Test
A strong vision must pass ALL seven:

1. **Specific enough to guide trade-offs**: Can the team use this vision to say "no" to feature requests? If the vision would justify any feature, it's too vague.
2. **Ambitious enough to inspire**: Does it describe a future meaningfully different from today? Does it stretch the team beyond current capabilities?
3. **Human-centered**: Describes the change in a user's life, not a product capability or business metric.
4. **Time-bounded**: Set 3-5 years out. Close enough to feel real, far enough to be aspirational.
5. **Memorable**: A team member can recall it without looking it up (ideally in one sentence or short paragraph).
6. **Falsifiable**: You could look back in 5 years and say "we achieved this" or "we didn't."
7. **Differentiated**: This vision could not describe your top competitor's product.

### Vision vs. Related Concepts

| Concept | Time Horizon | Purpose | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Mission** | Permanent | Why we exist | "Organize the world's information" |
| **Vision** | 3-5 years | What we're building toward | "Every knowledge worker has a research assistant that reads 1000x what they can" |
| **Strategy** | 12-18 months | How we'll make progress toward the vision | "Focus on legal researchers; win with citation accuracy" |
| **Goals/OKRs** | Quarterly | Measurable milestones | "Reach 10K WAU among legal professionals" |

### Anti-Patterns to Avoid

| Anti-Pattern | Example | Why It Fails |
|---|---|---|
| **Generic aspiration** | "Be the best platform for X" | Applies to every competitor; guides no decisions |
| **Feature list** | "A product that does A, B, and C" | Describes today's product, not the future |
| **Financial target** | "Reach $100M ARR" | Doesn't inspire; doesn't describe user value |
| **Buzzword soup** | "AI-powered next-gen solution" | Means nothing specific; no one can act on it |
| **Mission masquerading as vision** | "Empower businesses worldwide" | Too permanent; no endpoint; no specificity |
| **Internal focus** | "Be the fastest-growing product in the portfolio" | Users don't care about your internal metrics |

## Vision Formats

### Format 1: Narrative Vision (recommended for teams)
A 2-3 paragraph story told from a future user's perspective.

**Structure:**
- Paragraph 1: Set the scene. Who is this person? What is their context?
- Paragraph 2: Show how they use the product. What's different about their experience?
- Paragraph 3: Describe the outcome. How is their work or life meaningfully better?

**Example:**
"It is 2029. Maria is a product manager at a growing fintech company. She opens her laptop on Monday morning and her product dashboard shows not just what happened last week, but why. Customer feedback from 15 channels has been automatically synthesized into 3 opportunity themes, each linked to specific cohort behaviors. She doesn't spend her morning reading Slack threads and Jira tickets -- she spends it making decisions. By 10am, she's validated a hypothesis, updated her roadmap, and shared a data-backed recommendation with her VP. She goes home at 5pm feeling like a strategist, not an inbox manager."

### Format 2: One-Sentence Vision
For presentations and alignment documents.

**Template**: "[User type] can [do what differently] so that [meaningful outcome in their life]."

**Example**: "Every product team can make customer-backed decisions in minutes instead of weeks, so they ship the right things and stop guessing."

### Format 3: Press Release Vision (Amazon Working Backwards)
A fictional press release from 3-5 years in the future announcing what the product has achieved.

**Structure:**
1. **Headline**: The achievement in customer-centric terms
2. **Subheading**: Who benefits and what changed
3. **Problem paragraph**: What was broken before
4. **Solution paragraph**: How the product solved it
5. **Customer quote**: A fictional customer describing the impact
6. **How it works**: Brief description of the approach
7. **CEO/founder quote**: Why this matters at a company level
8. **Call to action**: How to get started

**FAQs to include:**
- Customer FAQs: pricing, setup, who it's for, how it compares
- Internal FAQs: feasibility, cost, timeline, dependencies, risks

### Format 4: Product Vision Board (Roman Pichler)
Structured canvas format:

```
PRODUCT VISION BOARD

VISION: [Overarching purpose -- the change you want to see in the world]

TARGET GROUP     | NEEDS            | PRODUCT           | BUSINESS GOALS
Who are the      | What problems    | What standout     | What business
target users?    | does it solve?   | features or       | outcomes do we
                 | What benefits    | capabilities      | expect?
                 | does it deliver? | does it offer?    |
```

### Format 5: Vision Deck (for leadership alignment)
5-7 slide structure:
1. **The world today**: What's broken and why it matters
2. **The opportunity**: What has changed that makes a new approach possible
3. **The vision**: What the future looks like (narrative format)
4. **How we get there**: 3-5 strategic pillars or phases
5. **What changes for users**: Before/after comparison
6. **What this means for the business**: Scale and impact
7. **The first step**: What we're doing THIS quarter toward the vision

## Process

### Step 1: Gather Inputs
Before crafting a vision, understand:
- Who is the primary user? (Specific, not generic)
- What is the most important problem or job they face?
- What is the current state of the product? (What exists today?)
- What macro trends create an opportunity? (Technology, behavior, regulation)
- What are the constraints? (Team size, market position, resources)
- What existing vision language exists? (Improve, don't ignore)

### Step 2: Apply First Principles (Elon Musk Approach)
Challenge assumptions about the current state:
- "Why does this problem exist?" -- keep asking why until you reach fundamental truths
- "What would the ideal solution look like if we had no constraints?"
- "What would have to be true for this to work?"
- "What assumptions are we making that might be wrong?"

### Step 3: Work Backward from the Future (Bezos Approach)
- Imagine it's 5 years from now and the vision is fully realized
- What is the user doing differently?
- What is no longer true that is true today?
- What would a journalist write about this product?

### Step 4: Draft the Vision
Choose the format that fits the context and draft.

### Step 5: Stress-Test the Vision
Apply these tests:
- **The decision test**: Name 3 features the team would reject based on this vision
- **The uniqueness test**: Could a competitor use this exact vision? If yes, it needs sharpening
- **The elevator test**: Can someone repeat it from memory after hearing it once?
- **The inspiration test**: Would a new hire feel excited after reading this?
- **The specificity test**: Remove all adjectives. Does it still mean something?

### Step 6: Offer Alternatives
Always present 2-3 alternative phrasings or framings, each emphasizing different aspects (user value, market position, technology approach).

## Vision-to-Strategy Cascade
A vision without execution is a hallucination. Connect the vision to action:

```
VISION (3-5 years): [The future state]
  |
  STRATEGY (12-18 months): [The bets and trade-offs to move toward the vision]
    |
    PRINCIPLES (permanent): [Decision rules derived from the vision]
    |  e.g., "We always prioritize accuracy over speed"
    |  e.g., "We design for teams, not individuals"
    |
    ROADMAP (this quarter): [What we're building next]
      |
      SPRINT GOALS: [This sprint's contribution]
```

### Deriving Principles from Vision
A good vision generates 3-5 product principles that guide daily trade-off decisions:
- If the vision emphasizes simplicity: "When in doubt, remove features rather than add them"
- If the vision emphasizes collaboration: "Every feature should work better with 2+ people than alone"
- If the vision emphasizes data accuracy: "We never show uncertain data without confidence indicators"

## Aligning Teams Around Vision

### Vision Workshop (90 minutes)
1. **Share context** (15 min): Current state, market trends, user research
2. **Individual drafting** (15 min): Each participant writes their version of the vision
3. **Gallery walk** (15 min): Post all visions; silent reading and dot-voting on resonant phrases
4. **Synthesis** (30 min): Combine strongest elements into 2-3 candidate visions
5. **Stress-test** (15 min): Apply the 5 tests to each candidate; select or iterate

### When to Revisit the Vision
- Major market shift (new competitor, technology disruption, regulatory change)
- Company strategy pivot
- Vision achieved or no longer ambitious
- New leadership with different perspective
- Annual strategy review (review, don't necessarily change)
