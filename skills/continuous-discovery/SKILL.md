# Continuous Discovery Guide

Based on Teresa Torres' Continuous Discovery Habits framework. Continuous discovery is the ongoing practice of making small research touchpoints with customers each week to inform product decisions.

## The Core Loop

Apply this skill to: $ARGUMENTS


Discovery is not a phase - it is a weekly cadence:
1. Talk to at least one customer per week (weekly interviews)
2. Continuously synthesize insights into an Opportunity Solution Tree
3. Map assumptions before building anything
4. Design the smallest experiment to test the riskiest assumption
5. Ship, learn, update

## The Opportunity Solution Tree (OST)

The OST is a visual map that connects:

```
Desired Outcome (the measurable business goal)
    |
    Opportunities (unmet user needs, pain points, or desires)
        |
        Solutions (product ideas that address the opportunity)
            |
            Experiments (tests to validate the solution before building)
```

### Building the OST

**Level 1 - Desired Outcome**: One specific, measurable business outcome the team is responsible for. Not "improve engagement" but "increase weekly active users who complete at least 3 core actions from 34% to 45% by Q3."

**Level 2 - Opportunities**: Customer needs, pain points, and desires discovered through interviews. Each opportunity should be stated in the customer's language, not in product terms. "I lose track of what my team is working on when I'm traveling" is an opportunity. "Add a mobile dashboard" is a solution, not an opportunity.

**Level 3 - Solutions**: Product ideas, features, or changes that address a specific opportunity. One opportunity should have multiple solution candidates.

**Level 4 - Experiments**: The smallest, cheapest test to validate the key assumption behind each solution.

## Opportunity Framing

A well-framed opportunity has three properties:
1. It is stated from the customer's perspective
2. It contains a clear pain, need, or desire
3. It does not imply a specific solution

Test: Can you imagine multiple different solutions that address this opportunity? If only one solution fits, the opportunity is probably too narrow.

## Assumption Mapping

Before committing to a solution, map its assumptions across four risk categories:

| Risk Type | Question to Ask |
|---|---|
| Desirability | Do customers actually want this? |
| Usability | Can customers figure out how to use this? |
| Viability | Does this work for our business model? |
| Feasibility | Can we build this with our current capabilities? |

Prioritize assumptions by: Impact (if wrong, does the whole solution fail?) x Evidence (how much do we already know?). Test the high-impact, low-evidence assumptions first.
