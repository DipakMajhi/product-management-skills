# Pricing Strategy Advisor

Pricing is a strategic lever, not just a financial calculation. You help PMs think through pricing from value, psychology, and competitive angles.

## Pricing Strategy Framework

Apply this skill to: $ARGUMENTS


### Step 1: Understand Value Delivered
Before setting a price, quantify the value the product delivers:
- **Economic value**: Time saved, revenue generated, cost reduced, risk eliminated
- **Emotional value**: Confidence, status, ease of mind
- **Competitive displacement value**: What would the customer pay for the next best alternative?

### Step 2: Understand Willingness-to-Pay
Methods to estimate willingness-to-pay:
- **Van Westendorp**: Ask customers at what price the product would be too cheap (low quality), acceptable cheap, good value, and too expensive
- **Conjoint analysis**: Present trade-off scenarios to identify what features justify price increases
- **Champion/challenger testing**: Test two price points with comparable user groups
- **Benchmark to alternatives**: What does a customer currently pay (time + money) to solve this problem?

### Step 3: Choose a Pricing Model
See the monetization models table from the business-model skill. For pricing specifically:
- **Value-based pricing**: Price based on value delivered (highest margin potential)
- **Cost-plus pricing**: Cost to serve + margin (simple but leaves money on the table)
- **Competitive pricing**: Anchor to market rates (safe but undifferentiating)
- **Penetration pricing**: Price below market to gain share, then raise (high risk)
- **Skimming**: Launch high, reduce over time (works for limited early adopters)

### Step 4: Design the Tier Structure
For multi-tier pricing (Starter / Pro / Enterprise):
- Each tier should map to a clearly differentiated user segment
- Upgrade triggers should be natural pain points, not artificial limitations
- The middle tier is usually where most revenue comes from - make it compelling
- Enterprise tier should justify a sales conversation

### Step 5: Pricing Psychology
- Anchor with the highest tier first (top-down presentation)
- Use charm pricing ($99 not $100) for consumer-facing products
- Highlight the "most popular" tier to reduce choice paralysis
- Express pricing in terms of cost per outcome ("$5 per hour saved") when ROI is high
