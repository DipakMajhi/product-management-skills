# Value Proposition Designer

A value proposition is a clear statement of the specific value your product delivers to a defined customer. It answers: who benefits, from what, how, and compared to what alternative. A great value proposition makes the right customers say "that's exactly what I need" and the wrong customers say "that's not for me."

## The Strategyzer Value Proposition Canvas

### Customer Profile (understand first)

**Jobs to Be Done:**
- Functional jobs: What practical tasks are they trying to accomplish?
- Emotional jobs: How do they want to feel? (Confident, in control, creative, relieved)
- Social jobs: How do they want to be perceived? (Competent, innovative, responsible)
- Supporting jobs: Related tasks around the main job (buying, onboarding, maintaining, disposing)

**Pains:**
- What frustrates them about current solutions?
- What risks do they fear? (Financial, social, technical)
- What obstacles prevent them from getting the job done?
- What mistakes do they commonly make?
- Which pains are most severe? (Rank by intensity, not just frequency)

**Gains:**
- What outcomes do they desire beyond the basic job?
- What would delight them unexpectedly?
- What would make their life or work significantly better?
- What metrics define success for them?
- Which gains are most important? (Rank by value, not just desirability)

### Value Map (design second)

**Products and Services:**
- What specific capabilities does your product offer?
- Which jobs does each capability address?

**Pain Relievers:**
- How does your product eliminate or reduce specific pains?
- Which pains do you address most effectively?
- How completely do you relieve each pain? (Fully, partially, superficially)

**Gain Creators:**
- How does your product create specific gains?
- Which gains do you deliver most effectively?
- Do you create any unexpected gains (delighters)?

### Fit Assessment
- **Problem-Solution Fit**: Do your pain relievers and gain creators address the jobs, pains, and gains that matter most?
- **Product-Market Fit**: Do customers demonstrate willingness to pay and return for your solution?

## The 6-Part JTBD Value Proposition Framework

A complete value proposition answers six questions:

| Component | Question | Specificity Test |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Who** | Who specifically benefits? | Not "enterprises" but "HR directors at Series B-D software companies with 50-200 employees" |
| **2. Why (the pain)** | What specific problem do they face? | Can you quote a user describing this pain? |
| **3. Current situation** | What do they do today instead? | What specific alternative are you displacing? |
| **4. How (the solution)** | What does your product do differently? | Could a competitor claim the same thing? |
| **5. What changes (the outcome)** | What improves in their work or life? | Is this measurable or observable? |
| **6. Why you (the differentiator)** | Why is your solution meaningfully better? | Does this pass the "so what?" test three times? |

## Positioning Strategy (April Dunford)

Choose your positioning approach based on market context:

### Strategy 1: Head-to-Head
Win an existing market by outcompeting the established leader.

**When to use**: You have a genuinely superior product on dimensions the market already values.
**Risk**: Requires significant resources to out-market an incumbent.
**Example**: "Like [Competitor], but faster/cheaper/more accurate."

### Strategy 2: Big Fish, Small Pond
Win a market subsegment by specialization.

**When to use**: You can't beat the leader broadly but can dominate a specific niche.
**Risk**: Niche may be too small. May be difficult to expand later.
**Example**: "The [category] built specifically for [niche segment]."

### Strategy 3: Create a New Game
Define and win a new market category.

**When to use**: Your product doesn't fit existing categories well. Existing categories constrain how people understand your value.
**Risk**: Highest investment in education. Market may not materialize.
**Example**: "We're not a [existing category]. We're the first [new category]."

### Dunford Positioning Process
1. **Identify unique attributes**: What can you do that nobody else can?
2. **Translate to value themes**: What does each attribute mean for the customer?
3. **Identify target customers**: Who cares most about these value themes?
4. **Determine market context**: Which context (Head-to-Head, Big Fish, New Game) makes your value most obvious?
5. **Layer on emotional and social value**: Beyond functional -- how does it make them feel and look?

## Competitive Displacement Analysis

Understand what you're really competing against:

| Alternative Type | What It Is | How to Displace |
|---|---|---|
| **Direct competitor** | Same category, similar features | Win on specific value dimensions (speed, accuracy, price, UX) |
| **Indirect competitor** | Different solution, same job | Reframe the job; show limitations of their approach |
| **Manual process** | Spreadsheets, email, manual workarounds | Quantify the cost of the manual approach (time, errors, scale) |
| **Do nothing** | Customer doesn't address the problem | Quantify the cost of inaction (lost revenue, risk, opportunity cost) |
| **Internal tool** | Company-built solution | Highlight maintenance burden, opportunity cost, and capability gaps |

**Displacement question**: "What would have to be true for this customer to stop using [current alternative] and switch to us?" The answer reveals your real value proposition.

## Value Proposition Testing

### Qualitative Testing
Show the value proposition to 5-10 target users and ask:
- "What do you think this product does?" (Comprehension)
- "Who do you think this is for?" (Targeting accuracy)
- "Would you want to learn more?" (Interest)
- "What questions do you have?" (Gaps in the proposition)
- "How is this different from [alternative]?" (Differentiation clarity)

### Quantitative Testing
- **Landing page A/B test**: Test different value propositions as hero copy; measure click-through and signup rates
- **Ad copy testing**: Run multiple ad variants with different value propositions; measure CTR and conversion
- **Sean Ellis PMF survey**: "How would you feel if you could no longer use this product?" (40%+ "very disappointed" = PMF)

### Iteration Triggers
Revisit the value proposition when:
- Conversion rates decline without product changes
- Competitor messaging shifts and resonates
- User research reveals a new or shifted primary job
- Product capabilities significantly change
- Target market expands or contracts

## Value Proposition Statement Formats

### Format 1: Elevator (for presentations and pitches)
"For [WHO] who [WHY -- pain/goal], [Product] is a [category] that [HOW -- what you do differently]. Unlike [CURRENT SITUATION -- alternative], we [WHY YOU -- key differentiator]."

### Format 2: Hero Copy (for landing pages)
**Headline**: [The outcome the user gets -- outcome-first, not feature-first]
**Subheadline**: [How you deliver that outcome and who it is for]
**Supporting bullets** (3 max): [Key benefits with proof points]
**CTA**: [Specific action aligned to the value]

### Format 3: Sales Narrative (30-second version)
"Most [who] struggle with [pain]. They try [current solution] but [why it fails]. We help [who] [achieve outcome] by [how]. Unlike [alternative], we [differentiator]."

### Format 4: Investor Version
"[Market size] [who] spend [current cost/time] on [pain]. [Product] eliminates this by [how], delivering [quantified outcome]. We win because [moat]. We've achieved [traction proof point]."

### Format 5: Internal Alignment Version
"We exist to help [who] [outcome]. We do this by [approach]. We're different because [differentiator]. We measure success by [metric]. We don't serve [explicit non-target]."
