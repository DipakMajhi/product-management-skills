# Stakeholder Management Toolkit

You help PMs build systematic stakeholder maps and communication plans, navigate difficult stakeholder dynamics, and manage cross-functional relationships effectively.

Apply this skill to: $ARGUMENTS

## Step 1: Stakeholder Mapping

### Framework 1: Power/Interest Grid (Mendelow's Matrix)

Place each stakeholder on a 2x2 matrix:

| | High Interest | Low Interest |
|---|---|---|
| **High Power** | **Manage Closely**: Key partners; involve in decisions, frequent updates, build relationship | **Keep Satisfied**: Inform proactively, do not overwhelm with detail, engage when their area is affected |
| **Low Power** | **Keep Informed**: Transparent updates, leverage their enthusiasm, potential future advocates | **Monitor**: Minimal engagement, periodic updates, watch for changes in power or interest |

For each stakeholder, assess:
- **Power**: Ability to impact the project (approve/block budget, resources, scope, hiring, strategy)
- **Interest**: Level of personal stake in the outcome (career impact, team impact, customer impact)

**Dynamic nature**: Stakeholder positions shift as projects evolve. Re-map at major milestones.

### Framework 2: Stakeholder Salience Model (Mitchell, Agle, Wood)

Assess three attributes to determine stakeholder priority:

| Attribute | Definition | Assessment Question |
|---|---|---|
| **Power** | Ability to influence outcomes | Can they approve, block, or redirect this project? |
| **Legitimacy** | Appropriateness of their claim or involvement | Do they have a valid stake in this decision? |
| **Urgency** | Time sensitivity and criticality of their needs | Is their need time-sensitive? Will delay cause harm? |

### Stakeholder Classification by Salience

| Type | Attributes | Priority | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Definitive** | Power + Legitimacy + Urgency | Highest -- address immediately | CEO requesting a compliance fix before audit deadline |
| **Dominant** | Power + Legitimacy | High -- regular engagement | VP of Engineering with budget authority |
| **Dangerous** | Power + Urgency (no legitimacy) | High -- manage carefully | Executive pushing a pet feature with no user evidence |
| **Dependent** | Legitimacy + Urgency (no power) | Medium -- advocate for them | End users reporting a critical bug |
| **Dormant** | Power only | Low -- monitor for activation | Board member not currently engaged |
| **Discretionary** | Legitimacy only | Low -- engage as needed | Industry analyst following your space |
| **Demanding** | Urgency only | Low -- do not let them derail priorities | Vocal community member with a niche request |

## Step 2: Decision Frameworks (RACI and DACI)

### RACI Matrix

| Role | Definition | Rule |
|---|---|---|
| **Responsible** | Does the work | Can be multiple people |
| **Accountable** | Owns the outcome, approves the output | Only ONE person per decision |
| **Consulted** | Input sought before the decision (two-way) | Subject matter experts |
| **Informed** | Notified after the decision (one-way) | Affected parties |

### DACI Matrix (Intuit Model)

| Role | Definition | Rule |
|---|---|---|
| **Driver** | Drives the decision forward, gathers input, keeps process moving | PM or project lead |
| **Approver** | Makes the final call | ONE person with authority |
| **Contributors** | Provide knowledge, data, or work product | SMEs, designers, engineers |
| **Informed** | Need to know the outcome | Affected teams, leadership |

### When to Use Each

| Framework | Best For |
|---|---|
| RACI | Operational task management (who does what for ongoing work) |
| DACI | Specific decisions (go/no-go, prioritization, architecture choices) |

### DACI Example

| Decision | Driver | Approver | Contributors | Informed |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Feature prioritization for Q3 | PM | VP Product | Engineering Lead, Design Lead, Data Analyst | Sales, CS, Marketing |
| Pricing model change | PM | CEO | Finance, Marketing, Sales Lead | Engineering, CS |
| Technical architecture for new service | Engineering Lead | CTO | PM, DevOps, Security | Design, QA |

## Step 3: Communication Plan Design

### Communication Cadence by Stakeholder Type

| Stakeholder Type | Frequency | Format | Content Focus | Owned By |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Manage Closely** (High Power, High Interest) | Weekly or bi-weekly | 1:1 meeting or brief written update | Progress, risks, decisions needed | PM |
| **Keep Satisfied** (High Power, Low Interest) | Monthly or at milestones | Executive summary email or brief | High-level status, outcomes, strategic alignment | PM or PM's manager |
| **Keep Informed** (Low Power, High Interest) | Bi-weekly or monthly | Team newsletter, async update, or Slack channel | Progress, upcoming changes, opportunities to contribute | PM or designated team member |
| **Monitor** (Low Power, Low Interest) | Quarterly or as needed | Broadcast update or all-hands mention | Major milestones only | PM |

### Stakeholder Alignment Techniques

| Technique | How It Works | When to Use |
|---|---|---|
| **Pre-wiring** | Share your proposal with key stakeholders individually before the group meeting | Before any decision meeting; major presentations should culminate conversations, not start them |
| **One-on-ones** | Regular 1:1 cadence with high-power stakeholders to build relationship and gather input | Ongoing with Manage Closely stakeholders |
| **Stakeholder newsletter** | Brief async update summarizing progress, decisions, and upcoming milestones | When stakeholder group is large (10+) |
| **Decision log** | Shared document recording what was decided, by whom, and why | After every major decision; prevents revisiting settled issues |
| **Demo / show-and-tell** | Live demonstration of progress to build excitement and alignment | At sprint boundaries or major milestones |
| **Feedback windows** | Defined periods where stakeholders can provide input before a decision closes | Before finalizing roadmap, designs, or prioritization |

## Step 4: Managing Up (Working with Executives)

| Principle | Practice |
|---|---|
| **Dependability** | Announce what you will do, then announce when you have done it. Meet commitments. |
| **Proactive ownership** | Always have a proposal for next steps. Let executives course-correct, not initiate. |
| **Strategic alignment** | Understand what matters most to your executive. Frame updates in their language and priorities. |
| **Crisis communication** | Over-communicate during problems. Executives worry most about unknown bad news. |
| **Earned autonomy** | Build trust through transparency and delivery. Autonomy is granted, not assumed. |
| **Efficient communication** | Executives have limited time. Lead with the conclusion, then provide supporting context. |

### Executive Update Format

```
Subject: [Product] Weekly Update -- [Date]

STATUS: [On Track / At Risk / Blocked]

Key Progress:
- [Achievement 1 and its business impact]
- [Achievement 2 and its business impact]

Decisions Needed:
- [Decision 1]: [Options and recommendation] -- Need by [date]

Risks:
- [Risk 1]: [Mitigation plan]

Metrics:
- [Key metric]: [Current value] vs. [Target] ([trend])
```

## Step 5: Difficult Stakeholder Archetypes

| Archetype | Behavior | Strategy |
|---|---|---|
| **The Scope Creeper** | Continuously adds requirements after agreement | Make the cost of each addition visible: "Happy to add that -- it moves launch from March to May. Should we deprioritize something else?" |
| **The Absent Approver** | Critical decision-maker who is never available | Use "unless I hear otherwise by [date], I will proceed with [plan]." Document and send in writing. |
| **The Skeptic** | Does not believe the product will succeed; says so publicly | Invite them into the process. Skeptics with high power need a proof point, not more persuasion. |
| **The Micromanager** | Wants to approve every decision, including small ones | Over-communicate proactively. Send brief updates before they ask. Build trust, then earn autonomy. |
| **The WoLF** (Works on Latest Fire) | Creates constant disruption and context-switching with urgent requests | Establish a triage process. Not every fire is real. Ask: "What is the business impact if we address this next week instead of today?" |
| **The RHiNO** (Really High-value New Opportunity) | Sales/marketing pushing for specific features based on deals | Use a consistent prioritization framework. Show the opportunity cost: "Building X means not building Y. Which has higher expected value?" |
| **The Bottleneck** | Perpetually busy; slows everything down | Reduce their involvement to the minimum necessary. Offer to prepare materials that make their review faster. Escalate if chronic. |
| **The HiPPO** (Highest Paid Person's Opinion) | Overrides data and team judgment with personal preference | Bring data to every discussion. Frame as "the data suggests X" rather than "I think X." If they still override, document and measure the outcome. |
| **The Passive Resister** | Agrees in meetings but does not follow through or subtly undermines | Address directly in 1:1. Ask: "I noticed X did not happen after our last discussion. Is there a concern I am missing?" |
| **The Cautious Blocker** | Valuable for critical thinking but uses caution to delay or prevent action | Channel their caution constructively: "What would need to be true for you to be comfortable proceeding?" |

### General Principles for Difficult Stakeholders

1. Assume positive intent first. Most difficult behavior comes from misaligned incentives, not malice.
2. Understand their constraints. What pressures are they under that you do not see?
3. Find shared ground. Identify the outcome you both want and work backward.
4. Document agreements. Reduce ambiguity about what was decided.
5. Escalate when needed. Pushing for clarity is the PM's job. Thoughtful escalation is not weakness.

## Step 6: Escalation Framework

### When to Escalate

| Situation | Escalation Appropriate? |
|---|---|
| Stakeholder blocks a decision after multiple alignment attempts | Yes |
| Two stakeholders disagree and neither will yield | Yes |
| Resource conflict between teams that PM cannot resolve | Yes |
| Stakeholder overrides data with opinion on a high-stakes decision | Yes, after documenting data |
| Minor disagreement on implementation approach | No -- resolve directly |
| Stakeholder has a valid concern you have not addressed | No -- address the concern first |

### How to Escalate

1. **Document the situation**: What was decided, who disagrees, what attempts were made to resolve
2. **Propose options**: Present 2-3 paths forward with trade-offs (do not just bring a problem)
3. **Identify the decision-maker**: Who has authority to resolve this?
4. **Schedule a conversation**: Do not escalate via email for complex issues
5. **Follow up**: Document the resolution and communicate to all affected parties

## Step 7: PM Interview Preparation (STAR Format)

For "Tell me about a time you managed a difficult stakeholder":

| Component | Allocation | Content |
|---|---|---|
| **Situation** | 20% | Context: company, product, project, stakeholder role |
| **Task** | 10% | Your responsibility and the specific challenge |
| **Action** | 60% | YOUR personal actions: how you understood their concerns, what you tried, how you adapted |
| **Result** | 10% | Positive outcome: alignment achieved, project succeeded, relationship improved |

### Key Interview Tips

- Use "I" statements (not "we") to show your personal contribution
- Show empathy: demonstrate you understood the stakeholder's perspective
- Include a specific data point or artifact (email, dashboard, presentation) you used
- Demonstrate adaptability: show that your first approach did not work and you adjusted
- End with what you learned and how it changed your approach going forward

### Example Structure

"At [Company], I was leading [initiative]. The VP of Sales was concerned about [specific issue] because [their perspective]. Initially I tried [first approach], but that did not address their core concern. So I [adapted action -- pulled data, scheduled 1:1, created shared framework]. This resulted in [positive outcome]. I learned that [insight about stakeholder management]."
