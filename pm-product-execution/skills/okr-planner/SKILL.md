# OKR Planner

You write focused, measurable OKRs that actually drive behavior and create accountability.

## OKR Fundamentals

Apply this skill to: $ARGUMENTS


**Objective**: A qualitative, inspirational statement of what you want to achieve. It should be:
- Memorable and motivating (not "Improve product")
- Directional but not prescriptive
- Achievable within the OKR cycle (quarter or half)

**Key Results**: 2-4 quantitative measures that define what success on the Objective looks like. Each KR should:
- Start with a verb (Increase, Decrease, Launch, Achieve)
- Have a clear metric with a from-X-to-Y format where possible
- Be measurable without ambiguity at the end of the period
- Represent an outcome, not a task ("Increase weekly active users from 50K to 70K" not "Build new dashboard")

## The Ambitious Calibration

OKRs are meant to be ambitious. A score of 0.7 out of 1.0 at the end of the cycle is considered a success. If you always score 1.0, your KRs were not ambitious enough. If you always score 0.3 or below, they were unrealistic.

## Common OKR Mistakes

- **Task KRs**: "Launch feature X by March 15" is a milestone, not a KR. Add the outcome: "Launch feature X by March 15, increasing D7 retention by 8 percentage points"
- **Vanity KRs**: Metrics that look good but do not indicate real progress ("Publish 10 blog posts")
- **Too many KRs**: More than 4 KRs per Objective loses focus
- **Activity vs outcome**: "Run 20 customer interviews" is an activity. "Identify 3 validated opportunities from 20 customer interviews" is an outcome-adjacent KR

## Cascading OKRs

When team OKRs must align with company or department OKRs:
1. Start from the company/dept OKR
2. Identify: what must the product team contribute for this company KR to be achieved?
3. Define the team OKR that is the team's specific contribution
4. Ensure the team's KR, if achieved, would meaningfully move the company KR
